Part 1 · 0:00 – 0:30

Seaborn

statistical plots, one line each

Same data, more story

Seaborn sits on top of Matplotlib. It knows about DataFrames, categorical variables, and statistical aesthetics by default.

sns.scatterplot(data=penguins, x='bill_length_mm',
                y='bill_depth_mm',
                hue='species', style='sex')

Five plots that earn their keep

  • scatterplot — relation, colored by category.
  • boxplot — per-group distribution summary.
  • violinplot — boxplot plus KDE shape.
  • pairplot — every numeric vs every numeric.
  • heatmap — correlation or any matrix.

Correlation heatmap — five lines, big insight

corr = penguins.select_dtypes('number').corr()
sns.heatmap(corr, annot=True, cmap='coolwarm', center=0)

First thing to do on any wide numeric dataset. Reveals collinearity in one glance.

Part 2 · 0:30 – 1:00

SciPy stats

Distributions are objects

stats.norm.pdf(x, loc=0, scale=1)
stats.norm.cdf(x)
stats.t.cdf(x, df=3)
stats.chi2.cdf(x, df=3)

Every distribution exposes .pdf, .cdf, .ppf, .rvs, .fit. Same API for Normal, t, chi-squared, gamma, beta.

Fit a Normal in one call

mu, sigma = stats.norm.fit(samples)

Maximum-likelihood by default. Overlay the fitted PDF on a histogram to eyeball goodness.

One-sample t-test

res = stats.ttest_1samp(samples, popmean=0)
res.statistic, res.pvalue

Use when you want to know whether a sample's mean differs significantly from a hypothesized value. We will lean on this in Week 3.

Part 3 · 1:00 – 1:30

Optimize + Grand Finale

minimize — the engine under every model

res = optimize.minimize(rosen, x0=[0.0, 0.0])
res.x       # argmin
res.fun     # min value
res.nit     # iterations used

Every supervised model is "define a loss, call an optimizer." You just used both halves.

The 1-D loss landscape

f = lambda x: (x - 3)**2 + np.sin(5 * x)
res = optimize.minimize(f, x0=0.0)

A 1-D plot is the cheapest sanity check you have. Always look at your loss surface when something feels off.
