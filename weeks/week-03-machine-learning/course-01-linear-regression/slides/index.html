Part 1 · 0:00 – 0:30

Fitting a line

least squares, by hand

What is statistical learning?

We observe a response Y and predictors X = (X₁, …, X_p). We believe there is some unknown rule:

Y = f(X) + ε

f is the systematic information X contains about Y. ε is irreducible noise — measurement error, unmeasured causes, plain luck. Statistical learning is the set of tools for estimating f.

The best possible predictor at a point x is the regression function f(x) = E[Y | X = x] — the average Y among all units with that x. Linear regression assumes that average is a straight line.
Pull this directly from ISLR §2.1. The split between reducible (better f̂) and irreducible (the ε floor) is the mental model students should leave with.

Reducible vs irreducible error

Reducible

The gap between our f̂ and the true f. We close it with more data, better features, smarter models.

Irreducible

Var(ε). No model — no matter how clever — can predict Y better than f(X). This is the ceiling.

Even if we knew f exactly, we would still miss Y by ε. That's why test error never goes to zero.

Picture: Sales vs TV

    
Imagine a scatter of 200 dots. TV budget on the x-axis, Sales on the y-axis. The cloud slopes upward — clearly more TV money tends to correspond to more units sold, but with scatter.

    
Through that cloud we draw the line that minimizes the sum of squared vertical gaps between dots and line. That line is our estimated f̂.

    
The vertical gaps are the residuals. Their sum of squares is the loss we minimize.

  

  

    
Least squares = minimize the sum of squared verticals

    
    
ISLR Fig 3.1. OLS picks the line that makes the sum of squared red segments as small as possible.

  

  

    
See the residuals being minimized

    
Fit a line on 30 noisy points and draw the vertical residuals with ax.vlines. See the residuals being minimized.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  

  

    
The cost we are minimizing


Given (x₁, y₁), …, (xₙ, yₙ), the line ŷ = β₀ + β₁ x that minimizes residual sum of squares:
RSS(β) = Σᵢ (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁ xᵢ)²
Take the gradient, set to zero, two-line closed-form solution.

    
Loss + closed-form solution. The point: every later model just changes the loss or the model class.

  

  

    
RSS contours in (β₀, β₁)

    
    
ISLR Fig 3.2. The loss surface is an elliptic bowl; OLS sits at the bottom — the unique closed-form minimum.

  


  

    
Closed form — both coefficients

    
β̂₁ = Σ (xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) / Σ (xᵢ−x̄)², β̂₀ = ȳ − β̂₁ x̄. Two summations, no iteration. Run it by hand on the inlined Boston slice.

Three numbers you always report


    
  
  • R² — fraction of variance the model explains (0 = nothing, 1 = perfect).
    • 
  
  • RMSE — root mean squared error, in the units of y.
    • 
  
  • Residual plot — fitted vs (y − ŷ). Looks like noise? You are done. A pattern? The model is wrong somewhere.

R² as variance explained

    
    
R² is the share of the y-variance bar painted blue. RMSE measures how thick the gray slice is, in y's units.

Inference for β

    
is the slope real?

  

  

    
Standard error and the t-statistic

    
t = β̂₁ / SE(β̂₁). If |t| is big (typically > 2) the slope is reliably non-zero. Sklearn doesn't report this — compute it directly from the residuals.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  


  

    
The bootstrap — a CI for anything

    
Resample rows with replacement, recompute the slope, repeat 2000 times, take the middle 95%. No distributional assumptions — just trust the sample looks like the population.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  

  

    
Bootstrap distribution of the slope

    
    
Resample, refit, repeat. The middle 95% of the recomputed slopes is your CI — no normal theory required.

  

  

    
The bootstrap distribution

    
Histogram of the 2000 resampled slopes, with the point estimate marked. Plot output isn't rendered here — copy this into Jupyter to see the figure.

Bias-variance, a first look

    
Imagine sweeping a knob from "very rigid" (a straight line) to "very flexible" (a curve that wiggles through every point). Plot training error and test error against that knob.

    
    
Training error always falls with flexibility. Test error is U-shaped: underfit on the left, overfit on the right. Course 4 makes this precise.

  

  

    
KNN regression — a non-parametric contrast

    
Prediction at a new x: average y over its K nearest neighbors. No formula for f, no β to learn.

    
It works beautifully in 1–2 dimensions. But in high dimensions, neighbors stop being near.

    
Curse of dimensionality. In p = 1, a 10% neighborhood spans 10% of the line. In p = 10, capturing 10% of the data needs a neighborhood that spans 10^(1/10) ≈ 80% of each axis. Your "neighbors" are no longer near.

    
Direct quote from ISLR §3.5. Use it to motivate why linear/parametric models are still the default at high p.

  

  

    
Four assumptions of linear regression


    
  
  • Linearity — residual plot has no curve.
    • 
  
  • Constant variance — no fan, no funnel.
    • 
  
  • Independence — observations not autocorrelated.
    • 
  
  • Normality of errors — Q-Q plot of residuals is straight.
    • 


  

  

    
Q-Q plot intuition

    
    
Points hug the line in the middle, drift off at the tails — classic signature of residuals with heavier-than-normal tails.

  

  

    
Q-Q plot of residuals

    
A quantile-quantile plot checks normality of residuals — points on a straight line mean the normality assumption holds. Plot output isn't rendered here — copy this into Jupyter to see the figure.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  






  

    Part 4 · 1:30 – 2:00
    
Multiple regression

    
Y = Xβ + ε

  

  

    
Advertising — three media at once

    
Going from Sales ~ TV to Sales ~ TV + Radio + Newspaper. Fitted on the same 200 markets:

    

      
CoefficientStd. Errort-statp-value

      
Intercept2.9390.31199.42< 0.0001

      
TV0.0460.001432.81< 0.0001

      
Radio0.1890.008621.89< 0.0001

      
Newspaper-0.0010.0059-0.180.8599

    

    
In the simple regression Sales ~ Newspaper, the newspaper slope was significant. Here, controlling for TV and Radio, it collapses to essentially zero. Newspaper spending is correlated with Radio spending — markets that buy radio also buy newspaper — but newspaper has no independent contribution.

    
This is THE example for "marginal vs joint significance." Never read a coefficient without asking what else is in the model.

  

  

    
Is anything related to Y at all? — the F-statistic

    
F = [(TSS − RSS)/p] / [RSS/(n − p − 1)]

    
On Advertising (n = 200, p = 3): F ≈ 570. Under the null that all three slopes are zero, F is around 1. A value of 570 is overwhelming evidence that at least one medium matters.

    
Why not just look at the individual p-values? With p = 100 predictors and pure noise, about 5 will look significant at the 0.05 level. The F-test gives a single global verdict.

  

  

    
Mosteller & Tukey's puzzle

    
"In a regression of football tackles on weight and height, the coefficient on height comes out negative. Does that mean tall players make fewer tackles?"

    
No. Among players of equal weight, the taller ones tend to be the lankier ones — different positions, different physiques. The coefficient is the effect of height holding weight fixed, not the marginal effect of height.

    
Every coefficient in multiple regression is a "holding other things constant" coefficient. Forget that and you misread every model.

  

  

    
The matrix form


Y = Xβ + ε,  β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀ Y
One predictor or one hundred — the formula is the same. The expensive bit is the matrix inverse, but n ≪ 10⁴ → microseconds.

Collinearity flips signs

    
Variance Inflation Factor: for predictor j, fit x_j ~ rest, get R²_j, then VIF_j = 1/(1 − R²_j). Anything above 5–10 is collinear with the rest of the design. On Boston, tax, rad, nox are the biggest offenders — coefficient signs in collinear models can flip when you add or drop a feature.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  


  

    
Collinearity ellipse — why estimates wobble

    
RSS contours in (β₁, β₂) space. When predictors are correlated, the contours stretch into a long, tilted ellipse.

    
    
Estimates slide along the long axis with little change in RSS ⇒ unstable coefficients. ISLR Fig 3.15 / 6.7.

  


  

    
VIF bar chart — who is collinear?

    
Variance Inflation Factor per predictor. Rules of thumb: VIF > 5 worth a look, VIF > 10 trouble.

    
    
Sort descending; investigate or drop / combine anything past the red line.

  


  

    
Coefficients are unstable when predictors are correlated

    
Generate x2 = x1 + small noise (heavily collinear), then fit two LinearRegressions on the same x's but with slightly different noise in y. Watch the β vectors flip sign even though the underlying signal is identical. Then compute VIF.

Diagnostic plots

    
the 2×2 panel

  

  

    
The four plots


    
  
  • Residuals vs fitted — look for a curve (model misspecified).
    • 
  
  • Q-Q plot — look for tails (heavy-tailed errors).
    • 
  
  • Scale-location — look for a fan (heteroscedasticity).
    • 
  
  • Residuals vs leverage — look for high-leverage outliers (Cook's distance).
    • 


  


  

    
R's 2×2 diagnostic panel — at a glance

    
The same four mini-plots R prints when you call plot(lm.fit). Train your eye to scan them in five seconds.

    
  


  

    
Leverage in action — one point can swing the slope

    
A single high-leverage observation at the far right pulls the OLS line toward itself.

    
    
High leverage = far from the mean of x. One such point can swing the slope dramatically.

  


  

    
Cook's distance — bubble plot

    
x = leverage, y = standardized residual, bubble size = Cook's D. Points past D = 0.5 deserve a look; past D = 1, action.

The TV × Radio synergy

    
On Advertising, adding a TV·Radio interaction term:

    
Sales = β₀ + β₁·TV + β₂·Radio + β₃·(TV·Radio) + ε

    
    
      
  • Without interaction: R² = 0.897.
    • 
      
  • With interaction: R² = 0.968.
    • 
      
  • Interpretation: each extra dollar on radio is more valuable when TV spending is high — the two media reinforce each other.
    • 
    

    
Hierarchy principle. When you include TV·Radio, keep TV and Radio as main effects too — even if their t-stats become small. Otherwise the model is hard to interpret and not invariant to rescaling.

  

  

    
Credit data — qualitative predictors

    
The Credit dataset: predict Balance from Income, Limit, Rating, Cards, Age, Education, plus four categoricals — Gender, Student, Married, Ethnicity.

    
For a binary like Student ∈ {Yes, No}, create one dummy:

    
D = 1 if Student = Yes, else 0

    
The fitted coefficient on D is the average extra balance carried by students, holding everything else fixed. The baseline level (No) is absorbed into the intercept.

    
For Ethnicity with 3 levels you create 2 dummies. Always one fewer than the number of levels — otherwise perfect collinearity with the intercept (the dummy-variable trap).

  

  

    
Interactions with a qualitative predictor

    
Balance ~ Income, split by Student status. Two ways to model it:

    

      

        
No interaction

        
Two parallel lines: students sit a fixed amount above non-students at every income.

        
Balance = β₀ + β₁·Income + β₂·Student

      

      

        
With interaction

        
Two lines with different slopes: the marginal effect of income on balance differs for students vs non-students.

        
+ β₃·(Income · Student)

      

    

    
If lines visibly fan out or cross, parallel-line model is wrong. Add the interaction.

  


  

    
Interaction effects — parallel vs crossing

    
Left: no interaction — the effect of x is the same for both groups. Right: interaction — group changes the slope.

    
  


  

    
When linear isn't enough — Auto mpg

    
Scatter of mpg against horsepower in the Auto data: a clear downward curve. A straight line systematically over-predicts at the extremes and under-predicts in the middle — its residual plot has a giant U-shape.

    
Fitting mpg = β₀ + β₁·hp + β₂·hp² straightens out the residuals and lowers test MSE substantially.

    
Still linear in β, so the same closed-form fit. We just engineered a new column. Course 2 generalizes this.

  


  

    
Polynomial wiggle — high degree, Runge phenomenon

    
Same 12 points, four polynomial fits. Degree 15 explodes at the tails — classic overfit.

    
    
High degree ⇒ Runge phenomenon: small data wiggles become huge swings at the edges. Course 4 uses regularization to tame this.
