Part 1 · 0:00 – 0:30

Multiple regression

Y = Xβ + ε

Advertising — three media at once

Going from Sales ~ TV to Sales ~ TV + Radio + Newspaper. Fitted on the same 200 markets:

CoefficientStd. Errort-statp-value
Intercept2.9390.31199.42< 0.0001
TV0.0460.001432.81< 0.0001
Radio0.1890.008621.89< 0.0001
Newspaper-0.0010.0059-0.180.8599
In the simple regression Sales ~ Newspaper, the newspaper slope was significant. Here, controlling for TV and Radio, it collapses to essentially zero. Newspaper spending is correlated with Radio spending — markets that buy radio also buy newspaper — but newspaper has no independent contribution.

This is THE example for "marginal vs joint significance." Never read a coefficient without asking what else is in the model.

Is anything related to Y at all? — the F-statistic

F = [(TSS − RSS)/p] / [RSS/(n − p − 1)]

On Advertising (n = 200, p = 3): F ≈ 570. Under the null that all three slopes are zero, F is around 1. A value of 570 is overwhelming evidence that at least one medium matters.

Why not just look at the individual p-values? With p = 100 predictors and pure noise, about 5 will look significant at the 0.05 level. The F-test gives a single global verdict.

Mosteller & Tukey's puzzle

"In a regression of football tackles on weight and height, the coefficient on height comes out negative. Does that mean tall players make fewer tackles?"

No. Among players of equal weight, the taller ones tend to be the lankier ones — different positions, different physiques. The coefficient is the effect of height holding weight fixed, not the marginal effect of height.

Every coefficient in multiple regression is a "holding other things constant" coefficient. Forget that and you misread every model.

The matrix form

Y = Xβ + ε, β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀ Y

One predictor or one hundred — the formula is the same. The expensive bit is the matrix inverse, but n ≪ 10⁴ → microseconds.

Collinearity flips signs

If predictor j is well predicted by the others, its coefficient becomes unstable.

VIFj = 1 / (1 − R²j)

VIF > 5–10 is a red flag. Drop one of the correlated pair, or use Ridge (Course 6) to stabilize.

Run VIF in the notebook on Boston; tax, rad, nox are biggest offenders.

Never read a coefficient in isolation

  • Its sign depends on which other predictors are in the model.
  • Its size depends on the scale of the predictor.
  • Its significance depends on collinearity with the others.
Part 2 · 0:30 – 1:00

Diagnostic plots

the 2×2 panel

The four plots

  • Residuals vs fitted — look for a curve (model misspecified).
  • Q-Q plot — look for tails (heavy-tailed errors).
  • Scale-location — look for a fan (heteroscedasticity).
  • Residuals vs leverage — look for high-leverage outliers (Cook's distance).

Leverage — the hat matrix

H = X(XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀ, hᵢ = Hᵢᵢ

A high hᵢ means observation i has unusual predictor values — it pulls the fit toward itself. Combined with a big residual = Cook's distance = trouble.

Part 3 · 1:00 – 1:30

Categoricals & interactions

one-hot and products

One-hot encoding

df = pd.get_dummies(carseats,
    columns=['ShelveLoc','Urban','US'],
    drop_first=True, dtype=float)

drop_first=True avoids the dummy-variable trap (perfect collinearity with the intercept).

Interactions are just products

df['Price_x_Urban'] = df['Price'] * df['Urban_Yes']

The coefficient on the product term tells you how the effect of Price changes when Urban switches from No to Yes.

The TV × Radio synergy

On Advertising, adding a TV·Radio interaction term:

Sales = β₀ + β₁·TV + β₂·Radio + β₃·(TV·Radio) + ε

  • Without interaction: R² = 0.897.
  • With interaction: R² = 0.968.
  • Interpretation: each extra dollar on radio is more valuable when TV spending is high — the two media reinforce each other.
Hierarchy principle. When you include TV·Radio, keep TV and Radio as main effects too — even if their t-stats become small. Otherwise the model is hard to interpret and not invariant to rescaling.

Credit data — qualitative predictors

The Credit dataset: predict Balance from Income, Limit, Rating, Cards, Age, Education, plus four categoricals — Gender, Student, Married, Ethnicity.

For a binary like Student ∈ {Yes, No}, create one dummy:

D = 1 if Student = Yes, else 0

The fitted coefficient on D is the average extra balance carried by students, holding everything else fixed. The baseline level (No) is absorbed into the intercept.

For Ethnicity with 3 levels you create 2 dummies. Always one fewer than the number of levels — otherwise perfect collinearity with the intercept (the dummy-variable trap).

Interactions with a qualitative predictor

Balance ~ Income, split by Student status. Two ways to model it:

No interaction

Two parallel lines: students sit a fixed amount above non-students at every income.

Balance = β₀ + β₁·Income + β₂·Student

With interaction

Two lines with different slopes: the marginal effect of income on balance differs for students vs non-students.

+ β₃·(Income · Student)

If lines visibly fan out or cross, parallel-line model is wrong. Add the interaction.

When linear isn't enough — Auto mpg

Scatter of mpg against horsepower in the Auto data: a clear downward curve. A straight line systematically over-predicts at the extremes and under-predicts in the middle — its residual plot has a giant U-shape.

Fitting mpg = β₀ + β₁·hp + β₂·hp² straightens out the residuals and lowers test MSE substantially.

Still linear in β, so the same closed-form fit. We just engineered a new column. Course 3 generalizes this.

Recap

Three things to leave with

  1. β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy, but always check VIF first.
  2. The 2×2 diagnostic panel is the first reflex. Five seconds tell you a lot.
  3. Categoricals one-hot, interactions multiply. Drop one dummy to avoid collinearity.
