Scaling & pipelines

StandardScaler, log1p

Why scale at all?

  • Plain LR doesn't care — but regularized LR does (Ridge/Lasso in Course 6).
  • Distance-based methods (KNN, SVM next week) crumble without scaling.
  • Neural nets converge faster on standardized inputs.

Why scale at all? — Auto column ranges

On the Auto dataset, weight ranges in the thousands while acceleration is in the teens. Any distance-based or regularized model will let weight dominate unless we standardize.

  


  

    
StandardScaler — center and unit-variance

    
Fit a StandardScaler on numeric columns. After transforming, each column has mean ~0 and std ~1 — putting weight and acceleration on equal footing.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  


  

    
Pipeline = scaler + estimator, one object

    
A Pipeline bundles preprocessing with the estimator so the scaler fits on training data only, surviving any future train/test split or cross-validation.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  


  

    
Skew? log1p it.

    
Right-skewed features (income, prices, horsepower) become symmetric under log1p(x) = log(1 + x), which handles zeros gracefully. Plot output isn't rendered here — copy this into Jupyter to see the figure.

Polynomial features turn LR into a curve-fitter


from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures
PolynomialFeatures(degree=3).fit_transform(X)
# [x] -> [1, x, x^2, x^3]
Linear in the parameters β, non-linear in x. Still linear regression!

  


  

    
The Wage data — age, year, education

    
3,000 male workers from the Mid-Atlantic. Predict wage from age, year, and education. ISLR's running example for non-linear modeling.

    
Picture the scatter of wage vs age: wages rise from age 18 to about 40, plateau through the 40s and 50s, then fall after 60. A straight line can only pick one of the three slopes.

    
Whenever the relationship is non-monotone, a single coefficient β₁ cannot describe it. We need a flexible representation of age as a feature.

  

  

    
Polynomial regression — degree 4 on wage ~ age

    
Replace age with [age, age², age³, age⁴] and fit by least squares.

    
The fit rises steeply, levels off around 45, dips after 60 — R² and residual plot both far better than the linear fit. Still linear regression, just on engineered columns.

    
Tail trouble. A degree-14 polynomial on wage looks fine in the middle but explodes near age 18 and 80. The fit at the boundary can be absurd.

  

  

    
Step functions — cut age into bins

    
Pick cut-points, say {35, 50, 65}. Define four dummy indicators:

    
C₁(age < 35),  C₂(35 ≤ age < 50),  C₃(50 ≤ age < 65),  C₄(age ≥ 65)

    
Fit a constant in each bin. The result is a staircase. Crude but interpretable — popular in epidemiology and credit scoring.

    
Problem: jumps at the cut-points. Reality doesn't jump at age 35. Solution: piecewise polynomials that match at the knots — splines.

  

  

    
From piecewise polynomials to splines

    
    
      
  • Piecewise polynomial — fit a cubic in each region, no continuity. Ugly jumps at knots.
    • 
      
  • Cubic spline — force the function and its first two derivatives to match at each knot. Smooth.
    • 
      
  • Natural cubic spline — additionally require linearity beyond the boundary knots. Tames the tails.
    • 
      
  • Smoothing spline — a knot at every observation, with a roughness penalty λ∫f''(x)² dx. Pick λ by CV.
    • 
    

    
For visualization, sklearn's SplineTransformer gives you natural cubic splines as plain features — fit ordinary LR on top.

    
The famous ISLR comparison: a degree-14 polynomial vs a natural cubic spline with the same df behave dramatically differently at the edges. Splines win.

  

  

    
GAMs — the generalization

    
A Generalized Additive Model writes:

    
y = β₀ + f₁(X₁) + f₂(X₂) + … + f_p(X_p) + ε

    
One smooth function per predictor, added up. For Wage:

    
wage = β₀ + f₁(year) + f₂(age) + f₃(education) + ε

    
Each f_j is fit as a spline. You get the flexibility of non-linear modeling and the interpretability of "see one curve per feature."

    
For classification: log-odds(wage > 250K) = β₀ + f₁(year) + f₂(age) + f₃(education). Same idea, logistic link.

  

  

    
Bias / variance, visualized


    
  
  • d = 1 — too straight (underfit, high bias).
    • 
  
  • d = 2 — captures the curve (just right).
    • 
  
  • d = 15 — wagging tails (overfit, high variance).
    • 

How do you pick d without eyeballing? Cross-validation, next course.

Encoding & interactions

    
ColumnTransformer is your friend

  

  

    
One-hot encoding — categories to columns

    
OneHotEncoder turns each category level into its own 0/1 column. drop='first' drops one level per feature to avoid the dummy-variable trap.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  


  

    
Ordinal encoding — only when categories have an order

    
OrdinalEncoder maps categories to integers in a user-specified order. Use it only when ordering is meaningful (e.g. low < medium < high), not for nominal categories where it would invent a fake order.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  


  

    
Different transforms for different columns

    
ColumnTransformer routes numeric and categorical columns through different preprocessors in a single expression, ready to drop into a Pipeline.

Interactions in bulk
