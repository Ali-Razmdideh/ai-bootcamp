Fitting a line

least squares, by hand

What is statistical learning?

We observe a response Y and predictors X = (X₁, …, X_p). We believe there is some unknown rule:

Y = f(X) + ε

f is the systematic information X contains about Y. ε is irreducible noise — measurement error, unmeasured causes, plain luck. Statistical learning is the set of tools for estimating f.

The best possible predictor at a point x is the regression function f(x) = E[Y | X = x] — the average Y among all units with that x. Linear regression assumes that average is a straight line.
Pull this directly from ISLR §2.1. The split between reducible (better f̂) and irreducible (the ε floor) is the mental model students should leave with.

Reducible vs irreducible error

Reducible

The gap between our f̂ and the true f. We close it with more data, better features, smarter models.

Irreducible

Var(ε). No model — no matter how clever — can predict Y better than f(X). This is the ceiling.

Even if we knew f exactly, we would still miss Y by ε. That's why test error never goes to zero.

Picture: Sales vs TV

    
Imagine a scatter of 200 dots. TV budget on the x-axis, Sales on the y-axis. The cloud slopes upward — clearly more TV money tends to correspond to more units sold, but with scatter.

    
Through that cloud we draw the line that minimizes the sum of squared vertical gaps between dots and line. That line is our estimated f̂.

    
The vertical gaps are the residuals. Their sum of squares is the loss we minimize.

  

  

    
Least squares = minimize the sum of squared verticals

    
    
ISLR Fig 3.1. OLS picks the line that makes the sum of squared red segments as small as possible.

The cost we are minimizing


Given (x₁, y₁), …, (xₙ, yₙ), the line ŷ = β₀ + β₁ x that minimizes residual sum of squares:
RSS(β) = Σᵢ (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁ xᵢ)²
Take the gradient, set to zero, two-line closed-form solution.

    
Loss + closed-form solution. The point: every later model just changes the loss or the model class.

  

  

    
RSS contours in (β₀, β₁)

    
    
ISLR Fig 3.2. The loss surface is an elliptic bowl; OLS sits at the bottom — the unique closed-form minimum.

  


  

    
Closed form — both coefficients

    
β̂₁ = Σ (xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) / Σ (xᵢ−x̄)², β̂₀ = ȳ − β̂₁ x̄. Two summations, no iteration. Run it by hand on the inlined Boston slice.

Three numbers you always report


    
  
  • R² — fraction of variance the model explains (0 = nothing, 1 = perfect).
    • 
  
  • RMSE — root mean squared error, in the units of y.
    • 
  
  • Residual plot — fitted vs (y − ŷ). Looks like noise? You are done. A pattern? The model is wrong somewhere.
    • 


  

  

    
R² as variance explained

    
    
R² is the share of the y-variance bar painted blue. RMSE measures how thick the gray slice is, in y's units.

R² is not the whole story


A model with R² = 0.95 can still be useless if:
    
  
  • Its residuals show a clear U-shape (model misspecified).
    • 
  
  • Its predictions extrapolate badly outside the training range.
    • 
  
  • The signal is dominated by one feature with a known data leak.

Inference for β

    
is the slope real?

  

  

    
Standard error and the t-statistic

    
t = β̂₁ / SE(β̂₁). If |t| is big (typically > 2) the slope is reliably non-zero. Sklearn doesn't report this — compute it directly from the residuals.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  


  

    
The bootstrap — a CI for anything

    
Resample rows with replacement, recompute the slope, repeat 2000 times, take the middle 95%. No distributional assumptions — just trust the sample looks like the population.


    

      
      
      
    

    

  

  

    
Bootstrap distribution of the slope

    
    
Resample, refit, repeat. The middle 95% of the recomputed slopes is your CI — no normal theory required.

Bias-variance, a first look

    
Imagine sweeping a knob from "very rigid" (a straight line) to "very flexible" (a curve that wiggles through every point). Plot training error and test error against that knob.

    
    
Training error always falls with flexibility. Test error is U-shaped: underfit on the left, overfit on the right. Course 4 makes this precise.

  

  

    
KNN regression — a non-parametric contrast

    
Prediction at a new x: average y over its K nearest neighbors. No formula for f, no β to learn.

    
It works beautifully in 1–2 dimensions. But in high dimensions, neighbors stop being near.

    
Curse of dimensionality. In p = 1, a 10% neighborhood spans 10% of the line. In p = 10, capturing 10% of the data needs a neighborhood that spans 10^(1/10) ≈ 80% of each axis. Your "neighbors" are no longer near.

    
Direct quote from ISLR §3.5. Use it to motivate why linear/parametric models are still the default at high p.

  

  

    
Four assumptions of linear regression


    
  
  • Linearity — residual plot has no curve.
    • 
  
  • Constant variance — no fan, no funnel.
    • 
  
  • Independence — observations not autocorrelated.
    • 
  
  • Normality of errors — Q-Q plot of residuals is straight.
    • 


  

  

    
Q-Q plot intuition

    
    
Points hug the line in the middle, drift off at the tails — classic signature of residuals with heavier-than-normal tails.

  

  

    
Q-Q plot of residuals

    
A quantile-quantile plot checks normality of residuals — points on a straight line mean the normality assumption holds. Plot output isn't rendered here — copy this into Jupyter to see the figure.
