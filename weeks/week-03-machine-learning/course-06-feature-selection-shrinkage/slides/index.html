Part 1 · 0:00 – 0:30

Ridge

L2 penalty

Ridge objective

minimize Σᵢ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² + α Σⱼ βⱼ²

  • α → 0: same as OLS.
  • α → ∞: every β shrinks to 0.
  • Scale matters: always standardize before Ridge.

Ridge paths on Credit

Standardize the 10 Credit predictors, fit Ridge for λ on a log grid from 10⁻² to 10⁵. Plot each β̂_j as a function of λ.

The picture: at λ ≈ 0, the coefficients sit at their OLS values — income, limit, rating large, student modest, others near zero. As λ grows, every coefficient is pulled smoothly toward zero. None reach exactly zero — Ridge always keeps all p predictors in the model.

Scale matters. Ridge penalizes Σβ_j². If income is in dollars and cards is a count, their β's live on wildly different scales — the penalty hits one ten thousand times harder than the other. Always standardize before Ridge. Plain OLS is scale-invariant; Ridge is not.

Bias-variance for Ridge

Sweep λ from 0 (= OLS) to ∞ (= all coefficients zero):

  • λ small — low bias, high variance. Coefficients flap from sample to sample.
  • λ large — high bias (shrinkage pulls us away from the truth), low variance.
  • Sweet spot — the test-MSE U-curve, again. Cross-validation finds it.

Ridge wins biggest when OLS is high-variance: many predictors, modest n, collinearity. On Credit the gain is small; in genomics with p > n it is decisive.

Coefficient paths

Sweep α on a log grid, fit Ridge at each value, and plot every βj versus α. All paths shrink toward zero, none reach exactly zero. Plot output isn't rendered here — copy this into Jupyter to see the figure.

Why does Lasso zero out, Ridge not?

    
Both minimize RSS subject to a penalty budget on the β's. The shape of the budget region is everything.

    
    
Orange ellipses = RSS contours; blue = budget. The optimum is where the smallest ellipse first touches the budget. The diamond has corners on the axes — contours hit them often, killing a coefficient. The disc has no corners.

  

  

    
Lasso is a feature selector


    
  
  • Survivor coefficients form an interpretable subset.
    • 
  
  • Use when you suspect many irrelevant features.
    • 
  
  • Use when you want a sparse model for production speed.
    • 

Comparison with forward stepwise: usually similar features. Lasso is faster and one-shot.

When Lasso beats Ridge, and vice versa

    

      

        
Lasso wins

        
When the truth is sparse — a handful of predictors carry the signal, the rest are noise.

        
Genomics: 5 genes out of 20,000 drive the phenotype.

      

      

        
Ridge wins

        
When the truth is dense — many predictors each contribute a small effect, none is zero.

        
Polygenic traits, returns models with many weak factors.

      

    

    
You usually don't know which world you're in. That's why Elastic Net — a convex combination — is the safe default.

PCR and PLS — a brief contrast

    

      

        
PCR

        
Run PCA on X. Regress y on the top M principal components. Unsupervised dimension reduction — never looks at y when choosing directions.

      

      

        
PLS

        
Same idea, but choose directions that are highly correlated with y. Supervised dimension reduction.

      

    

    
Both reduce p before regressing. Both can fail when the top components are not the predictive ones. In practice Ridge / Lasso / Elastic Net dominate them on most tabular data.

Elastic Net & pipelines

    
the modern default

  

  

    
Elastic Net = α (l1_ratio · L1 + (1 − l1_ratio) · L2)


    
  
  • Combines Lasso sparsity with Ridge stability.
    • 
  
  • Helps when features are correlated in groups.
    • 
  
  • l1_ratio = 1 → Lasso. l1_ratio = 0 → Ridge.
    • 


  


  

    
End-to-end pipeline + CV

    
Chain StandardScaler → PolynomialFeatures (interactions) → Lasso, then grid-search α with 5-fold CV. Lasso prunes the explosion of interaction terms down to a handful that actually help.
